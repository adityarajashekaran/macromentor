## 01-which-bmr-formula
**Which BMR formula should you use — Mifflin-St Jeor, Katch-McArdle, or Cunningham?**
Word count: 1050

Sources verified:
- Mifflin MD, St Jeor ST, et al. 'A new predictive equation for resting energy expenditure in healthy individuals.' Am J Clin Nutr. 1990 (PMID 2305711) - confirmed N=498, indirect calorimetry, R2=0.64 for fat-free mass alone, R2=0.56 for weight alone, R2=0.71 for full model
- Frankenfield D, Roth-Yousey L, Compher C. 'Comparison of predictive equations for resting metabolic rate in healthy nonobese and obese adults: a systematic review.' J Am Diet Assoc. 2005 - confirmed Mifflin-St Jeor within 10% of measured RMR in 82% of nonobese and 70% of obese adults, best of the equations compared
- Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics Evidence Analysis Library (andeal.org) - confirmed current recommendation of Mifflin-St Jeor as preferred RMR equation when body composition data is unavailable
- Cunningham JJ. 'A reanalysis of the factors influencing basal metabolic rate in normal adults.' Am J Clin Nutr. 1980 (PMID 7435418) - confirmed reanalysis of 223 subjects from Harris and Benedict's 1919 data, equation BMR = 500 + 22(LBM)
- Cunningham JJ. 'Body composition as a determinant of energy expenditure.' Am J Clin Nutr. 1991 (PMID 1957828) - confirmed equation REE = 370 +/- 21.6 x FFM, numerically identical to the Katch-McArdle formula
- ten Haaf T, Weijs PJM. 'Resting Energy Expenditure Prediction in Recreational Athletes of 18-35 Years: Confirmation of Cunningham Equation and an Improved Weight-Based Alternative.' PLOS ONE, 2014 - confirmed N=90 (53 men, 37 women), Cunningham equation within 10% of measured REE for 84.9% of men and 78.4% of women with ~1-2% bias by sex, and confirmed detail that Cunningham's 1991 sample was 239 subjects excluding 16 trained athletes
- O'Neill JER, Corish CA, Horner KM. 'Accuracy of Resting Metabolic Rate Prediction Equations in Athletes: A Systematic Review with Meta-analysis.' Sports Medicine, 2023 (PMC10687135) - confirmed 29 studies reviewed, Cunningham equation used in 21 of them (most of any equation), Cunningham derivation population 'omitted participants deemed athletic,' and that the Ten-Haaf (2014) equation was found most accurate/precise overall rather than Cunningham
- Wang Z et al. 'Specific metabolic rates of major organs and tissues across adulthood.' Am J Clin Nutr. 2010 (PMID 20962155) - confirmed organ-specific metabolic rates in kcal/kg/day: liver ~194, brain ~233, heart+kidney ~426, skeletal muscle ~12.6, adipose tissue ~4.4

Flagged for human review:
- The claim that Katch-McArdle has 'no original peer-reviewed validation study' and traces solely to the McArdle/Katch/Katch textbook could not be confirmed against a primary source (no such study surfaced in searches, but absence of evidence isn't proof of absence). Consistent with its absence from the systematic reviews checked, but worth a second look if a definitive citation is needed.
- The historical claim that the McArdle/Katch/Katch textbook 'picked up' Cunningham's 1991 regression and republished it under a new name is repeated across secondary/fitness sources but could not be verified by reading the textbook directly (not accessible). The numerical identity between the two formulas IS independently confirmed from Cunningham's own 1991 abstract.
- Added a caveat, not present in the original draft, that the 2023 Sports Medicine meta-analysis found a newer equation (Ten-Haaf, 2014) more accurate overall than Cunningham, and grouped Cunningham among five equations with no significant difference from measured values rather than singling it out as best. This is factually necessary but softens the piece's implicit case for Cunningham; worth a look to confirm it fits the intended editorial stance.
- The Frankenfield 2005 percentages (82% nonobese / 70% obese within 10% of measured RMR) were confirmed via secondary summaries and an evidence-library citation rather than by reading the full original journal article, which I could not access cleanly (the one copy found was a garbled PDF). Recommend a direct check against the original J Am Diet Assoc article if it's available to you.
- Removed the original draft's specific claim that the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics has recommended Mifflin-St Jeor 'since the mid-2000s' — I could not verify that adoption date and replaced it with an undated statement of the current recommendation.

## 02-1200-calorie-floor
**The 1,200-calorie floor: where it comes from, and when a calculator should refuse to go lower**
Word count: 1107

Sources verified:
- Jensen MD et al., 2013 AHA/ACC/TOS Guideline for the Management of Overweight and Obesity in Adults, Circulation (2014;129:S102-S138) — confirmed via full text: three alternative prescription methods are (1) 1,200-1,500 kcal/d women / 1,500-1,800 kcal/d men, (2) a 500- or 750-kcal/d deficit, (3) an evidence-based restrictive diet; these are listed as separate options, not one derived from another.
- NHLBI, Clinical Guidelines on the Identification, Evaluation, and Treatment of Overweight and Obesity in Adults (1998, NIH Publication 98-4083) — confirmed via direct PDF text extraction: recommends a 500-1,000 kcal/day deficit, with fixed-target option of 1,000-1,200 kcal/day for women and 1,200-1,500 kcal/day for men (lower than the 2013 guideline's numbers).
- NIH/NIDDK Body Weight Planner documentation — confirmed minimum modelable intake of 1,000 kcal/day, with a 'Dangerously Low Intake' warning below that.
- National Task Force on the Prevention and Treatment of Obesity, 'Very Low-Calorie Diets,' JAMA 1993;270(8):967-974 (PMID 8345648) — confirmed via abstract: VLCD defined as ≤800 kcal/day (3350 kJ/d), weight loss averaging 1.5-2.5 kg/week, 'serious complications of modern VLCDs are unusual, cholelithiasis being most common.'
- Damms-Machado A, Weser G, Bischoff SC, 'Micronutrient deficiency in obese subjects undergoing low calorie diet,' Nutrition Journal 2012;11:34 (PMID 22657586, PMC3404899) — confirmed via full text: baseline shortfalls in vitamin D, vitamin C, iron, folate, calcium and others; vitamin C and calcium deficiency rates worsened over the 3-month intervention despite a DRI-covering formula diet.
- Fothergill E et al., 'Persistent metabolic adaptation 6 years after The Biggest Loser competition,' Obesity (Silver Spring) 2016;24(8):1612-1619 — confirmed via full text: metabolic adaptation of -499±207 kcal/d at 6-year follow-up; 30-week original competition; authors explicitly note the 'extreme and public nature' of the intervention limits generalization to typical dieting.
- Johansson K, Sundström J, Marcus C, Hemmingsson E, Neovius M, 'Risk of symptomatic gallstones and cholecystectomy after a very-low-calorie diet or low-calorie diet in a commercial weight loss program,' 2014 — confirmed via abstract: 1-year matched cohort (3,320 per group), hazard ratio ~3.4 for gallstones requiring hospital care on VLCD vs LCD.
- Simpson CC, Mazzeo SE, 'Calorie counting and fitness tracking technology: Associations with eating disorder symptomatology,' Eating Behaviors 2017 — confirmed the paper exists and matches this title/topic via multiple bibliography listings; could not retrieve full abstract text directly (paywalled), so language was kept to what the title/topic supports rather than more specific claims.
- Helms ER, Aragon AA, Fitschen PJ, 'Evidence-based recommendations for natural bodybuilding contest preparation: nutrition and supplementation,' Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition 2014;11:20 — confirmed via full text: notes leaner competitors face greater risk of lean-mass loss for a given deficit, and cites Garthe et al.
- Garthe I, Raastad T, Refsnes PE, Koivisto A, Sundgot-Borgen J, 'Effect of two different weight-loss rates on body composition and strength and power-related performance in elite athletes,' International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism 2011 — confirmed via abstract: slow-rate group (0.7%/wk) gained lean body mass (+2.1%) and preserved strength; fast-rate group (1.4%/wk) had flat lean mass change; fat loss comparable between groups.
- Poon ET-C et al., 'Effects of intermittent dieting with break periods on body composition and metabolic adaptation: a systematic review and meta-analysis,' Nutrition Reviews (online 2024; print 2025;83(1):59-71) — confirmed via full text: 12 randomized trials, 881 participants; RMR significantly reduced with continuous energy restriction but not with break-inclusive dieting; no significant between-group difference in fat mass/body composition change.
- components/calculator/compute.ts (repo) — confirmed the app's actual floor logic: minCalories = 1200 for female, 1500 for male, matching the draft's product-behavior claims exactly.

Flagged for human review:
- Corrected a real conflation in the original draft: it attributed a '500 to 1,000 kcal/day' deficit AND the specific '1,200-1,500 women / 1,500-1,800 men' targets to the same 2013 AHA/ACC/TOS recommendation as if one derived from the other. In fact the 2013 guideline lists these as separate alternative prescription methods (deficit option is 500 or 750 kcal/day, not 500-1,000), and the 500-1,000 kcal/day deficit figure actually belongs to the 1998 NHLBI guideline, which used lower fixed-target numbers (1,000-1,200 for women, 1,200-1,500 for men) than the app's floor. Rewrote this section for accuracy — please double-check the rewritten framing reads clearly, since it's now more nuanced than the original.

However, the full abstract was paywalled and I could not directly quote its findings, so I could not verify the specific magnitude/direction of the correlation or its population (e.g., college students vs. general adults). I softened the original phrasing ('aggressive, unsupervised restriction... correlates with higher rates of disordered eating') to what I could actually confirm (app use correlating with higher ED symptom scores) — worth a human double-check against the actual paper if precision matters here.
- The 'breastfeeding needs run closer to 1,800 minimum' claim: this is a commonly repeated lay-nutrition figure, but I could not locate a primary authoritative source (ACOG, CDC, IOM/NASEM) confirming this exact number in the time available (search budget was exhausted mid-research). - The gallstone mechanism sentence ('fast fat breakdown pushes more cholesterol into bile than a calorie-starved, sluggish gallbladder can clear') is a simplified lay explanation of a real, more complex mechanism (increased cholesterol saturation of bile plus reduced gallbladder motility during rapid weight loss). It's directionally accurate but is a paraphrase, not a textbook-precise description — fine for a general-audience piece, flagging only so a clinical reviewer can confirm the simplification is acceptable.
