## A. NEAT: the ~2,000 kcal/day your activity dropdown flattens  *(highest priority)*

- **Target keywords:** non-exercise activity thermogenesis, NEAT, "why am I not losing weight in a
  deficit", NEAT calculator, how to increase NEAT.
- **Angle:** the single most under-appreciated variable in TDEE. NEAT (walking, fidgeting, posture,
  occupation, everything that isn't formal exercise) varies by up to ~2,000 kcal/day between
  individuals, and the research review argues NEAT variability matters MORE for TDEE accuracy than
  which BMR formula you pick. Ties directly to MacroMentor's activity-level input. Genuinely
  actionable: occupation type, standing, steps, and why the same "sedentary" label hides a huge
  spread. Also explains the frustrating "I'm in a deficit but stalled" experience (NEAT quietly
  drops when you diet).
- **Sourcing to chase:** James Levine's NEAT work (the ~2000 kcal/day figure and the overfeeding
  studies where NEAT determined who stored fat); adaptive NEAT reduction during energy restriction.
  Verify real PMIDs.

## B. Why BMI lies, and lies differently depending on your ancestry

- **Target keywords:** is BMI accurate, BMI for Asians, Asian BMI cutoff, BMI vs body fat, waist to
  height ratio (cross-link piece 04).
- **Angle:** BMI does not map to the same body-fat level across populations. The "Asian Indian
  phenotype" carries more body fat (and more visceral fat) at the same BMI; WHO uses lower BMI
  cutoffs for Asian populations for exactly this reason. Pairs with the waist-to-height-ratio piece
  (04) as the "here's the better tool" follow-through. Good honest-science hook: a number treated
  as universal that quietly isn't.
- **Sourcing to chase:** WHO expert consultation on Asian BMI cutoffs (2004); BMI-to-adiposity-by-
  ethnicity studies; Asian Indian phenotype literature. Verify PMIDs.

## C. Cold-adapted metabolism: the +7% that doesn't save you  *(science-curiosity; weaker calculator tie)*

- **Target keywords:** do cold climates increase metabolism, circumpolar metabolism, brown fat
  metabolism, does cold weather burn more calories.
- **Angle:** Indigenous circumpolar populations (Siberian, Inuit, Arctic) run measurably elevated
  BMR, roughly +3% to +19%, the best-evidenced population adjustment in the whole research review,
  via cold adaptation: enhanced thyroid response, brown adipose tissue, genetic + functional
  acclimatization. Then the honest turn: despite the higher resting burn, these groups face high
  obesity/diabetes rates (the "health paradox") because lifestyle transition dwarfs a few percent of
  BMR. Note: MacroMentor deliberately does NOT offer this as a calculator option (deferred product
  decision), so this reads more as a curiosity than a conversion piece, could also suit horizonfall.
- **Handle with care:** avoid biological determinism; frame as environmental/ancestral adaptation,
  not racial essentialism (same discipline as the ethnicity piece 03).
- **Sourcing to chase:** Leonard/Snodgrass circumpolar BMR studies; brown adipose tissue
  thermogenesis. Verify PMIDs.

## D. Does metabolism actually slow with age?  *(high-value, needs current lit)*

- **Target keywords:** does metabolism slow with age, metabolism by age, why is my metabolism slower,
  metabolism after 40.
- **Angle:** the counterintuitive current answer. The common belief (and the older framing in our
  own research doc) is that metabolism declines steadily from your 20s-30s. The landmark Pontzer et
  al. 2021 Science paper ("Daily energy expenditure through the human life course", large pooled
  doubly-labeled-water dataset) found that after adjusting for fat-free mass, metabolism is
  remarkably STABLE from ~age 20 to 60, then declines. Midlife weight gain is mostly about lean-mass
  loss and lifestyle, not a collapsing metabolic rate. Great differentiated piece because it
  corrects a near-universal myth.
- **MUST fact-check fresh:** this partly contradicts the pasted research doc's older "declines from
  20-30" framing. Lead with the Pontzer finding, verify it directly, and reconcile the age-decline
  language honestly. Also revisit whether MacroMentor's own age adjustment (the -1%/decade over 40
  in lib/calculator.ts) still reads as defensible in light of this, may be worth a note.
