# Quick Answers: TEF, BMR vs. RMR, and the Small Print of Your Calorie Number

A calorie calculator hands you one clean number, but a few honest questions sit just underneath it. Here are the short answers, the kind that clear up a nagging doubt without a lecture.

## Does protein really burn calories just by digesting it?

Yes, and it's a bigger effect than most people realize. Every time you eat, your body spends energy breaking food down, absorbing it, and putting it to use. That cost is called the thermic effect of food, and it accounts for roughly 10% of your total daily energy expenditure. The twist is that it isn't equal across the three macros. Protein has by far the highest thermic effect: a meaningful chunk of the calories in a protein-heavy meal goes straight to the cost of processing it, more than for carbs, and far more than for fat. It's one quiet reason higher-protein diets tend to help with fat loss and appetite, and part of why MacroMentor sets protein first when it builds your macro split. You don't get to eat protein for free, but you get closer than with anything else on the plate.

## BMR or RMR, and does the difference matter?

You'll see both terms, sometimes used as if they're identical. They aren't, quite. Basal metabolic rate is measured under strict lab conditions: first thing in the morning, fully rested, after an overnight fast, in a temperature-controlled room. Resting metabolic rate is measured under looser conditions, and it usually comes out about 10% higher. Most prediction formulas you'll meet online, Mifflin-St Jeor included, are really estimating something closer to RMR than true textbook BMR, even when they call it "BMR."

Here's the reassuring part: for planning your day, the distinction barely moves the needle. The far bigger sources of error in a TDEE estimate are picking a formula that doesn't fit your body and, most of all, misjudging your activity level. Nail those two and the BMR-versus-RMR hairsplitting won't change your target in any way you'd notice. What matters is that a calculator stays consistent with itself, using one definition all the way through, which is what MacroMentor does.

## So how much can I actually trust the number?

Treat it as a well-aimed starting point, not a verdict. Prediction equations are built on population averages, and real people scatter around those averages by a surprising amount, up to 25% between two individuals who look identical on paper. Your job isn't to hit the calculated number to the calorie. It's to start there, watch what actually happens to your weight and energy over two or three weeks, and adjust. A good calculator gets you close and shows its working. The feedback loop does the rest.
